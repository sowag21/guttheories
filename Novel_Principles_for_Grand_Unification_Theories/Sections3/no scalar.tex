The Standard model is chiral, meaning that left- and right-handed particles transform differently under the weak interaction, as we learned in Section \ref{sec:SM}. This prevents us from writing fermionic mass terms in the Lagrangian, resulting in the need to introduce a scalar to spontaneously break the gauge symmetry to generate massive fermions (and gauge bosons). Therefore, to better understand high energy behaviour of extensions of the SM, it is necessary to analyse the high energy behaviour utilising the CAF framework of Chapter \ref{chap:CAF} of chiral gauge-Yukawa theories with scalars.

In this chapter we will introduce two chiral models. The generalised Georgi--Glashow \cite{Georgi:1974yf} and the Bars--Yankielowicz \cite{Bars:1981se} models. In Section \ref{sec:noscalar} we consider both models separately without scalars, in the following section \ref{sec:fundscalar} we introduce a fundamental scalar, and in the last section \ref{sec:adj} we introduce an adjoint scalar. These two representations of scalars follow from the general need of a scalar to break the gauge group down to the SM gauge structure and one to play the role of the Higgs, as reviewed in Section \ref{sec:SU5}. 


\section{Introduction of the Chiral Models} \label{sec:noscalar}
The models we will consider consists both of vector-like fermions and chiral fermions transforming under the same non-Abelian gauge group $\SU(N)$. We will consider theories with three fermionic fields: the anti-fundamental fermion $\tilde\psi$, the fundamental fermion $\psi$, and the two-index fermion $\Lambda$. The two-index fermion will be chiral hence it has no anti fermionic field to write a mass term with. To extrapolate the models behaviours at high energies we need to compute beta functions, which are derived directly from the Lagrangian. 

The fundamental first step is to write down a Lagrangian that does not break the gauge symmetry or the global symmetries. The inclusion of $p$ and $q$ generations of vector-like fermions, naturally introduces two global flavour symmetries. The flavours of the vector-like fermions will affect the anomaly calculation of the gauge anomaly. To ensure a viable model, the gauge symmetry needs to be anomaly free, this is not automatically the case for Weyl fermions as it is for Dirac fermions \cite{Peskin:1995ev}. The anomaly arises from the triangle diagrams \cite{tong}, from which we obtain the following condition \cite{Peskin:1995ev}
\begin{align}
    \tr(T^a_R\{T^b_R,T^c_R\})=A(R)d^{abc}\,,\\\sum \mu_i A(R_i)\overset{!}{=}0\,,\label{eq:gaugeanomaly}
\end{align}
where $n_i$ is the multiplicity of each representations $R_i$ and $A(r)$ is the anomaly coefficient. The condition of the anomaly cancellation effectively introduces a relation between $p$, $q$, and the number of colours $N$.

Besides the two global flavour symmetries, the fields will also have two charges under two Abelian groups $\U_1(1)$ and $\U_2(1)$. Classically, one would expect that we would have three charges, since we have three fermionic fields. However, at the quantum level for $\SU(N)$ gauge groups the Adler--Bell--Jackiw (ABJ) anomaly \cite{Adler:1969gk,Bell:1969ts} will break one if these $\U(1)$ symmetries, and leaves us with $(3-1=2)$ independent, anomaly-free global $\U(1)$ symmetries \comment{check out my anomaly paper}. These charges also needs to be computed, and the Lagrangian will have to be charge neutral to not break the Abelian global symmetries of the model. 

The charges are assigned by demand that the ABJ anomaly coefficient vanishes. This is a similar procedure to that of the gauge anomaly cancellation. Originating from the mixed anomaly of the $\U(1)$ and $\SU(N)^2$ triangle diagram. The condition for a $\U(1)$ charge thus comes from \comment{derive this?}
\begin{align}
    \sum \mu_i Q_i T(R_i)\overset{!}{=}0\,,
\end{align}
where $T(R_i)$ is the Dynkin index of the representation. For our field configuration this leads to the expression
\begin{align}
     Q_\Lambda T(\Lambda) + p Q_\psi T(\psi) + q Q_{\tilde{\psi}} T(\tilde{\psi}) = 0\;,
\end{align}
inserting the Dynkin index of the the fundamental, and anti-fundamental representation the equation simplifies to 
\begin{align}
     2Q_\Lambda T(\Lambda) + p Q_\psi + q Q_{\tilde{\psi}} = 0\;.\label{eq:chargeeq}
\end{align}
To complete the charge assignment, one needs to choose two charges and choose the last one to ensure the anomaly cancellation. We will see this in the next section. 

Then the Lagrangian can be written. This is done in multiple steps. First, we consider the free Lagrangian. To do this we need to ensure that all terms will be invariant under $\U_1(1)$ and $\U_2(1)$ and that no indices remain open, which yields
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{free}}= i \bar \Lambda^{ij}\sigma^\mu \partial_\mu\Lambda_{ij}+i \bar \psi^i_\alpha\sigma^\mu \partial_\mu\psi_i^\alpha+i \bar {\tilde{\psi}}_i^\beta\sigma^\mu \partial_\mu{\tilde{\psi}}^i_\beta\;,
\end{equation}
where $\sigma^\mu$ is used because the fermions are chiral, and the following index conventions are adopted: The Lorentz index is denoted by $\mu$, the fundamental gauge indices from $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ are denoted by $i,j=1,\dots,N$, and the flavour indices are given by $\alpha=1,\dots, p$ and $\beta=1,\dots,q$. Next, we want to consider the interacting theory, which we do by promoting the global $\SU(N)$ symmetry of the Lagrangian to a local symmetry. To ensure gauge invariance we introduce the covariant derivative $\partial_\mu \to D_\mu= \partial_\mu - ig(T^a)^i_{\,j}A_\mu^a$
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_{\text{kin}}= -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu}^a F^{a\mu\nu}+ i \bar{\Lambda}^{ij} \slashed{D} \Lambda_{ij} 
    + i \bar{\psi}^i_\alpha \slashed{D} \psi_i^\alpha 
    + i \bar{\tilde{\psi}}_i^\beta \slashed{D} \tilde{\psi}^i_\beta \;,\label{eq:freeL}
\end{equation}
where $F_{\mu\nu}^a=\partial_\mu A_\nu^a-\partial_\nu A_\mu ^a+g f^{abc}A_\mu^bA_\nu^c$, with $A_\mu$ being the connection. The Lorentz indices are denoted by $\mu,\nu$ and the adjoint gauge index $a=1,\dots,N^2-1$. 

This gives us our first coupling constant, $g$. If we expand the first term of Eq. \ref{eq:freeL}, we get three types of terms
\begin{equation}
    F_{\mu\nu}^a F^{\mu\nu\,a}\sim(\partial_\mu A_\nu^a)^2+ g f^{abc}(\partial_\mu A_\nu^a)A_\mu^bA_\nu^c +g^2 f^{abc}f^{def}A_\mu^bA_\nu^cA_\mu^dA_\nu^e\;,\label{eq:generalL}
\end{equation}
which provides the gauge boson propagator, the three-point gauge boson vertex, and the four-point gauge vertex, respectively. The three remaining terms in $\mathcal{L}_{kin}$ yields the three fermion propagators, and their gauge interactions. 

This establish the Lagrangian of a general Chiral model with three fermionic fields. In the addition of scalar fields we need to consider both kinetic term of the field, the quartic self-coupling, and the interaction with the fermionic fields; the Yukawa couplings, which is needed to generate mass for the chiral fermions. This we will explore in Sections \ref{sec:fundscalar} and \ref{sec:adj}. However, to familiarise us with the models we will for now focus on the models with out scalars.

\subsection{Scalar-Free Georgi--Glashow model}
We start by considering a generalised Georgi--Glashow (GG) model \cite{Georgi:1974yf}. This model contains both chiral fermions and vector-like fermions. It therefore includes three fields: the anti-fundamental fermion $\tilde\psi$, the fundamental fermion $\psi$, and the two-index antisymmetric fermion $\Psi$. The three fermions all transform under $\mathrm{SU}(N)$. We will have $p$ copies of the fundamental fermion and $q$ copies of the anti-fundamental fermion. To ensure the gauge anomaly cancellation we will first utilise Eq. \eqref{eq:gaugeanomaly}
\begin{align}
    \sum \mu_i A(R_i)\overset{!}{=}0\,,\\(N-4)+p(1)+q(-1)=0\\q=N-4+p
\end{align}
where the we use the standard normalisation of the anomaly coefficients, letting the $A(\ytableausetup{boxsize=0.2cm, centertableaux}\begin{ytableau}{}\end{ytableau})=1$. Then the complex conjugate representation has the coefficient $A(\overline{\begin{ytableau}{}\end{ytableau}\rule{0pt}{6.5pt}})=-1$, and the two index anti-symmetric is $A(\begin{ytableau}{}\\{}\end{ytableau})=(N-4)$. Thus, we have two non-Abelian global symmetries $\SU(N-4+p)$ and $\SU(p)$. 

Next we need to assign the charges under $\U_1(1)$ and $\U_2(1)$. Utilising the Dynkin index of the two-index anti-symmetric fermion is $N=(N-2)/2$ and the generations of the anti-fundamental fermion $q=N-4+p$, we can use Eq. \eqref{eq:chargeeq} to write
\begin{align}
     Q_\Psi (N-2) + p Q_\psi + (N-4+p) Q_{\tilde{\psi}} = 0\;.
\end{align}
Now lets consider the charges of $\U_1(1)$, and let $Q_{1_\psi}=N+2$ and $Q_{1_{\tilde{\psi}}}=(N+2)$\footnote{These choices are \comment{...}}. This yields the charge of $Q_{1_\Psi}$
\begin{align}
     Q_{1_\Psi} (N-2) + p (N+2) - (N-4+p) (N+2) = 0\\
     Q_{1_\Psi}=N-4\,.
\end{align}
The same procedure is followed for $\U_2(1)$, where we take $Q_{2_\Psi}=2p$ and $Q_{2_{\tilde{\psi}}}=-p$, leaving us with
\begin{align}
     2p(N-2) + p Q_\psi - (N-4+p) p = 0\\
     Q_{2_\Psi}=-N+p\,.
\end{align}
Now that the global symmetries of the model has been specified, anomaly cancellation is ensured and charge assignment is complete, we can summarise the models transformation properties. The three fermions all transform under $\mathrm{SU}(N)$. The anti-fundamental fermion also transforms under the global symmetry group $\mathrm{SU}(N-4+p)$ and the fundamental fermion under $\mathrm{SU}(p)$. All fields are charged under the $\mathrm{U}_1(1)$ and $\mathrm{U}_2(1)$ symmetries. These transformation properties can be found in Table \ref{tab:GG}.

Since the GG model consist of a two-index anti-symmetric fermion there is a lower boundary on number of colours in the gauge group. This can be found by considering the dimensions of the representation given by $\frac{N(N-1)}{2}$ for different $N$. For $\SU(2)$, the dimensions is $1$. This results in a trivial singlet under the gauge group, thus not a chiral two-index anti-symmetric fermion. A similar conclusion can be found for $\SU(3)$, for which the dimensions would equal $3$. This is the dimensions of the (anti-)fundamental representation of the group. For $\SU(4)$, the dimension is $6$. However, for this group the $\mathbf{6}$ representation is real \cite{Georgi:1999wka}. A real fermion cannot be chiral because a bilinear invariant exists, and thus one can write a Majorana mass term to the Lagrangian\footnote{If the theory only includes one chirality of the field. Let us for this example say a left-handed fermionic field, then we cannot combine it with the right-handed field to write a Dirac mass. The only option is to introduce a Majorana mass term, this couples the left-handed fermion to itself \cite{Lancaster:2014} \begin{align} \mathcal{L}_{\text{mass}}\subset m(\psi^T\psi)+h.c.\,,\end{align}this term can only be written if the two left-handed fermions form a singlet under the gauge group. This is only the case for $\psi$ fields that transform as $R$ with $R=\bar{R}$. }. For the case of $N=5$ the dimension is $10$. The $\mathbf{10}$ representation is a complex anti-symmetric two-index representation, as seen in Section \ref{sec:SU5}. 
Thus, we have a lower boundary of the number of colours $N\ge5$ for the GG model.

In terms of these fields, we can write the Lagrangian using Eq. \eqref{eq:generalL}, which yields
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}= -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu}^a F^{a\mu\nu}+ i \bar{\Psi}^{ij} \slashed{D} \Psi_{ij} 
    + i \bar{\psi}^i_\alpha \slashed{D} \psi_i^\alpha 
    + i \bar{\tilde{\psi}}_i^\beta \slashed{D} \tilde{\psi}^i_\beta\;,\label{eq:lGGnoscalar}
\end{equation}
where we use similar index conventions as in Eq. \eqref{eq:freeL}: The Lorentz indices are denoted by $\mu,\nu$. The adjoint gauge index $a=1,\dots,N^2-1$, the fundamental gauge indices from $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ are denoted by $i,j=1,\dots,N$, and the flavour indices are given by $\alpha=1,\dots, p$ and $\beta=1,\dots,N-4+p$. 

This theory has one gauge coupling, with beta function \cite{Gross:1973ju}
\begin{equation}
    \beta_g=-{2\alpha_g^2}\left[\frac{11}{3}C_2(G)-\frac{2}{3}\sum_f T(R_f)-\frac{1}{3}\sum_s T(R_s)\right]\;, 
\end{equation}
\comment{do the calculation clearly of the gauge beta function, could use..Thus, when summing over the Dynkin index for all fermion representations, the anomaly-free expression of $\bar p $ is used 
\begin{equation}
    n_f T(R) = T(\Psi) + \bar p T(\bar{\square}) + p T({\square})=\frac{1}{2}\left(N\pm2 + N\pm 4 +p +p\right)=N+p\pm 3\;,
\end{equation}
where $T(\square)=T(\bar \square)$ is given by equation \ref{eq: Dynkin index fund. rep} and $T(\Psi)=\frac{N\pm2}{2}$, where $+$ is for symmetric and $-$ is for anti-symmetric representations. \comment{where does this come from} 
Now inserting this in the beta function, remembering that $n_f$ is the number of Dirac fermions, so we need to divide that term by two, when inserting the number of Weyl fermions 
\begin{equation}
    \beta_{\alpha_{g}}={-\alpha_g^2}\left[ \frac{22}{3}N-\frac{4}{3}N \mp \frac{12}{3}-\frac{4}{3}p\right]={-\alpha_g^2}\left[ \left(6-\frac{4}{3}x\right)N\mp 4\right]\;,
\end{equation}}
where the sums are taken over fermions $f$ and scalars $s$.
Since there are no scalars in the theory, the one-loop coefficient is given by:
\begin{equation}
b_0 = -2 \left[ \frac{11}{3} C_2(G) - \frac{2}{3} \sum_f T(R_f)\right] \;.
\end{equation}
Recall that the model contains the chiral fermion $\Psi$, which is in the anti-symmetric representation of $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ ($1$ copy) with dimensions $\frac{N(N-1)}{2}$. The vector-like fermion $\tilde{\psi}$ which is in the anti-fundamental representation ($N-4+p$ copies) with dimension $\bar{N}$. Lastly, $\psi$ which is in the fundamental representation ($p$ copies) with dimensions $N$.
Using the standard group factors for $\mathrm{SU}(N)$: $C_2(G) = N$, $T(\text{Fund}) = T(\text{Anti-Fund}) = 1/2$, and $T(\text{Anti-Symm}) = \frac{N-2}{2}$ and substituting in the field content, we obtain
\begin{align}
\sum T(R_f) &= 1 \cdot T(A) + (N-4+p) \cdot T(\bar{F}) + p \cdot T(F)  = N - 3 + p\;.
\end{align}
Therefore, the gauge beta function will depend on $N$ and $p$ as
\begin{align}
\beta_g &\propto \frac{11}{3}N - \frac{2}{3}(N - 3 + p) = \frac{9N + 6 - 2p}{3}\;.
\end{align}
Multiplying by the normalisation factor $(-2)$ from the definition in \eqref{eq:betagaugeformula}, we find
\begin{equation}
    \beta_g=-\frac{2\alpha_g^2}{3}\left({9}N+6-2p\right)\;. \label{eq:betagauge}
\end{equation}
The model contains only a single gauge coupling. Consequently, the theory is constrained solely by the condition on the gauge beta function coefficient, $b_0<0$. In this particular model, this translates into a constraint on the parameters $N$ and $p$, which can be rewritten as a function $p(N)$. This function describes the upper limit of vector-like fermions needed to be added in an $\mathrm{SU}(N)$ theory to ensure CAF, and is given by
\begin{equation}
    p_{\text{max}}(N)=\frac{9N+6}{2} \;.
    \label{eq:pNnoscalarGG}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Scalar-Free Bars--Yankielowicz model}
We now focus on the Bars--Yankielowicz (BY) model \cite{Bars:1981se}. This model is like the GG model a chiral model, the difference from the GG model is that the chiral fermion is in the two-index symmetric representation, i.e.\ $\chi_{ij}=\chi_{ji}$. This affects the anomaly coefficient of the field $A(\begin{ytableau}{}&{}\end{ytableau})=(N+4)$. This effectively changes the copies of the anti-fundamental vector-like fermion to $q=N+4+p$ to ensure gauge anomaly cancellation. This also changes the Dynkin index $T(\begin{ytableau}{}&{}\end{ytableau})$=(N+4)/2, effectively changing the computation of the charges and will introduce a sign change to $\U_1(1)$ charges, as seen in Table \ref{tab:BY}.

Similar to the GG model, the BY model also has a lower boundary of the number of colours of the gauge group. The dimensions of the two-index symmetric representation $\frac{N(N+1)}{2}$. For $N=2$ this yields a dimension of $3$, which is the adjoint representation of the group and thus is real \cite{Georgi:1999wka}. Thus, it is not a chiral fermion. For $\SU(3)$ the dimension is $6$ which is a complex representation. Resulting in a lower boundary of number of colours $N\ge3$ for the BY model. 

Thus, the Lagrangian is similar to that of the GG model and can be expressed as
\begin{align}\mathcal{L}= -\frac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu}^a F^{a\mu\nu}+ i \bar{\chi}^{ij} \slashed{D} \chi_{ij} 
    + i \bar{\psi}^i_\alpha \slashed{D} \psi_i^\alpha 
    + i \bar{\tilde{\psi}}_i^\beta \slashed{D} \tilde{\psi}^i_\beta
    \;,
\end{align}
with similar index definitions as in Eq.\ \eqref{eq:lGGnoscalar} except for the flavour index of the anti-fundamental fermion now given by $\beta=1,\dots, N+4+p$.

The gauge beta function can be found similarly to the GG model using Eq.\ \eqref{eq:betagaugeformula}. The computation only differs in the number of anti-fundamental fermions: $N+4+p$\footnote{The copies of anti-fundamental fermions $\tilde \psi$ change to ensure anomaly cancellation.}, and the Dynkin index of the chiral fermion $T(\text{Symm})=\frac{N+2}{2}$, which yields
\begin{align}
\sum T(R_f) &= \frac{N+2}{2} + \frac{N+4+p}{2} + \frac{p}{2} = N + 3 + p\;.
\end{align}
